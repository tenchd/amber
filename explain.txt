On June X, 2026, at roughly XXX UTC, I built a merkle tree from the raw text files of the works listed on Project Gutenberg and wrote the root hash of this merge tree to the Bitcoin blockchain in block XXX, or one of several blocks immediately following.
This document explains how to use the merkle tree I built, along with the blockchain transaction timestamping its root hash, to certify that a text file is an exact, unaltered copy of a literary work provided to the public by Project Gutenberg in 2026.
The same blockchain transaction also contains a cryptographically-secure SHA256 hash of this text file (the one you are reading right now) as well. This is the canonical version of the text file, and should be preserved exactly to ensure that it can be verified.

Motivation
The motivation for this work is to preserve a clear historical record of literary expression far into the future.
While the primary challenge of literary and historical conservation efforts across the centuries has been preserving texts, it has also been vital to identify forgeries because they obscure our view of the past.
If current technological trends continue, large-language models (LLMs) will drastically reduce the cost of creating convincing forgeries of historical texts.
In such a case, we can expect a massive flood of such forgeries to be created, for the same reasons that spam emails and malicious internet traffic are ubiquitous. 
Some of the forgeries will be made maliciously, by political, religious, or corporate actors that seek to rewrite inconvenient aspects of the historical record. More will be the result of error, misconfiguration, and laziness. 
Imagine the plight of our descendants two hundred years from now, who might wish to read a transcript of John Brown's remarks upon being found guilty for the crimes of treason and insurrection. Even if the true transcript survives, it may be indistinguishable among many convincingly altered versions. Intentional, politically-motivated alterations could include intensifying or removing Brown's appeals to Christianity, removing his denial of treasonous intent, or rewriting his professed abolitionist motivations into something cruder and more mercenary. Any one of these changes would fundamentally alter the text, and the historical understanding of any reader who accepted it as real. And how could our descendants know which is real, for this or any other text? What truths about our past we have managed to preserve would be lost to a totalizing cultural and historical dementia.
One possible way to avoid this distressing hypothetical future is to rely on a trusted institution, such as the Project Gutenberg organization, to maintain genuine copies of important works. However, we see in history that institutions often shift priorities, make mistakes, become influenced or undermined by outside forces, or even cease to exist. It would be better to have a means of re-establishing trust in the provenance of literary works, even after they have passed into the hands of untrusted parties.
To that end, the goal of this project is to provide cryptographic-strength, censorship- and tamper-resistant proof that each Project Gutenberg text existed today. Such proof would allow our hypothetical descendants in the above example to verify that the unaltered version of Brown's speech (which appears in its entirety in W. E. B. DuBois' biography "John Brown" on Project Gutenberg, PG62799) was, in fact, the version that existed in June 2026 and not a forgery produced later.
As I write this, there is still time to avert this concerning possible future. I do not yet see much evidence of proliferation of convincing forgeries. I suspect that, for the moment, creating a convincing forgery is still too inconvenient to be done at scale. But it's only a matter of time before this happens. If we do not prepare, we risk denying our descendants the irreplaceable gift of the literary record, from which we have benefitted for millenia. 

Scope
This work has three components: this document, the merkle tree, and the blockchain message. Taken together, they comprise a secure timestamp of the PG corpus.
My goal in providing this secure timestamp is:
- To provide cryptographic-strength, censorship- and tamper-resistant proof that each Project Gutenberg text existed today. This can be used to re-establish trust in the provenance of literary texts.

I do NOT intend this secure timestamp to:
- independently prove any Project Gutenberg work is authentic (not a forgery, correctly translated, complete, etc.).
- directly preserve the actual text of any Project Gutenberg work. The merkle tree stores hashes of the documents but not the documents themselves.

I assume the following conditions continue to hold in the future:
- Finding a SHA256 hash collision remains computationally infeasible.
- Genuine, unaltered versions of the raw text files hosted by Project Gutenberg survive.
- This document, and the accompanying Merkle tree, survive.
- The message I wrote on the Bitcoin blockchain containing the Merkle root hash and the hash of this document remain readable and provably unaltered (i.e., the blockchain is not edited in some kind of 51% attack).
- The Project Gutenberg index scheme stays the same (see below).

Trust in Provenance
Technically speaking, this secure timestamp establishes the fact that each included text file existed in June 2026, but nothing else. For example, they certify that a plain text document containing a novel titled Don Quixote (PG996) existed in June 2026, but do not conclusively prove that this is truly a novel published in the early seventeenth century by Miguel de Cervantes, or that it is a faithful and accurate translation into English from the original Spanish, or that it was obtained from the Project Gutenberg website.
This proof of existence alone is likely to be sufficient, because today it is still relatively expensive to produce convincing forgeries of literary works.
I have not been able to think of a way to provide a similar level of proof that the files I included in the Merkle tree are from Project Gutenberg and further that I did not alter them. I encourage my contemporaries to carefully test this secure timestamp on your own: download plain text files from Project Gutenberg and verify them with the merkle tree.

Merkle Tree Construction and Serialization
I constructed the Merkle tree of the Project Gutenberg plain text files in the same way a Bitcoin miner computes the Merkle root hash of the block of transactions. That is to say, let the unique integer identifiers of the works be the ordering of the files. First I compute the double SHA256 hash of each file. These comprise the leaves of the Merkle tree. Then for each adjacent pair of leaves in the ordering, I double hash the concatenation of their hashes to produce the hash of their parent node. The process is repeated at each level until the root is reached. If there is an odd number of nodes at some level, the last node's hash is concatenated with itself and then double SHA256 hashed to get the parent hash.

If you have two versions of this document which both point to different Bitcoin transactions, and both are valid, you should trust the one which appears earlier in the chain. This is because once I publish this document and merkle tree, it is easy for a malicious actor to make their own merkle tree with altered contents, and then repeat my process to timestamp their tree on the chain. In this case, their blockchain transaction would occur in a later block than mine.

Preservation and Dissemination
PG works are likely to survive even absent direct archival efforts because they are available for free and include many of the most popular and influential books in history. Ironically, another reason these works are likely to persist is because they are a curated collection of high-quality human-generated natural language, which is useful for training LLMs.
This document and merkle tree do not carry the same incentives to be preserved and used for other purposes. They are only useful or interesting insofar as they help establish trust in provenance of PG works. 
For that reason, please save this document and Merkle tree to your computer, without altering their contents. Back them up in multiple locations. Share them with other people. Print out a physical copy of this document and store it where it will last a long time. If you are a bookbinder, consider printing the hex dump of the Merkle tree as a bound book [insert calcuation of how mnay pages]. If you have access to other forms of long-term storage media, use that as well. 
Remember, even though future people may have no particular reason to trust you, if you manage to preserve a copy of the genuine versions of this document and the Merkle tree, they can verify them as genuine by referring to the blockchain message. The challenge is simply ensuring that these files persist into the future, so they can be used to reestablish trust in provenance if it has been lost. 
Finally, it may also be useful to download and store the Project Gutenberg raw text files yourself, as well. This is because Project Gutenberg has an errata process where small changes (typo corrections, etc) can be made to PG works. Since any change to the raw text file changes the hash signature, texts that are errata-ed after the creation of this Merkle tree can no longer be verified if the original is lost. By preserving the current state of PG works, you help ensure more of them will remain verifiable long into the future.

This is Not Hard To Do
Cryptographically-secure hashes and Merkle trees have been in use for decades. I am also not the first to establish proof of document existence via blockchain, though to my knowledge so far this technique has mostly been used in business and legal applications.

While the threat of cultural dementia is real, we already have the technical means to defend against it. But we must act soon. This Project Gutenberg secure timestamp is merely a first step. There is much more that can be done, including:
- supporting more types of data (audio, video, etc)
- working with existing conservation and archival organizations
- including works from more sources, world cultures, and languages
- building a volunteer, decentralized, resilient effort to preserve exact canonical versions of texts, as well as their secure timestamps
